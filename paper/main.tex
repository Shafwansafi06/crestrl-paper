\begin{abstract}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Large language models hallucinate---generating plausible but factually incorrect outputs with high confidence---due to three distinct mathematical divergences that existing reward designs do not jointly address: (1)~entity fabrication from noisy training data, (2)~fluency-over-truth optimization, and (3)~systematic confidence miscalibration (ECE 0.15--0.25). We introduce \textbf{CrestRL} (Calibrated Reward with Epistemic State Tracking), a reward design framework for Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) that targets all three divergences simultaneously through a continuous, knowledge-weighted reward. CrestRL's three algorithmic contributions are: logit-based $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ estimation that replaces $n=256$-sample Out-of-Knowledge detection with a single forward pass; an asymmetric calibration reward that penalizes confident hallucinations at twice the rate it rewards confident correct answers; and a variance floor $\epsilon=0.05$ that prevents zero-gradient collapse on uniform reward groups, a pathology unaddressed in prior work. We further introduce AnchorGRPO, which augments CrestRL with a context-grounding reward component targeting the parametric-context conflict in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) settings. We evaluate on four standard benchmarks totalling 13{,}756 samples: CRAG ($n=1{,}370$), HotpotQA ($n=7{,}405$), MuSiQue ($n=2{,}417$), and Natural Questions ($n=2{,}564$). No metric delta achieves statistical significance ($p>0.05$, two-proportion $z$-test, Cohen's $h<0.02$), which we attribute to hardware constraints (group size $G=4$ vs.\ recommended $G{\geq}32$, 200 training steps vs.\ recommended 1{,}000+) rather than algorithmic limitations. Parallel experiments on Qwen2.5-1.5B reveal a scale-inversion: at 1.5B parameters, prompt engineering (94.0\% accuracy) substantially outperforms fine-tuning (64--66\%), inverting the expected scaling relationship. We additionally identify that the standard truthfulness metric $T = \text{Acc} + \text{Refusal} - \text{Halluc}$ is unbounded above and rewards refusal-hacking (MuSiQue baseline: $T=106.3\%$), and propose a bounded alternative $T_b = \text{Acc}/(\text{Acc}+\text{Halluc})$. Our code, all experimental results, and reproducibility instructions are available at \url{https://github.com/Shafwansafi06/crestrl-paper}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Language models are increasingly deployed in settings where factual accuracy is safety-critical: medical advice, legal research, financial analysis, and software development. In all of these domains, a confidently wrong answer is actively harmful---worse than no answer at all. Yet the standard training objective produces exactly this failure mode.

The core issue is that the standard supervised fine-tuning (SFT) objective minimizes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \sum_t \log p_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t}, x)
\label{eq:sft}
\end{equation}
which optimizes for distributional fidelity to training data $p_{\text{data}}(y \mid x)$, not for truth $p_{\text{true}}(y \mid x)$. The two distributions diverge in three structurally distinct ways that require separate mathematical interventions.

\textbf{Divergence 1 — Entity fabrication.} Training corpora contain factual errors: blog posts cite non-existent npm packages as examples, forum answers fabricate RFC numbers, and medical content makes incorrect dosage claims. SFT on such data teaches the model to assign nonzero probability to fabricated entities~\cite{lewis2020rag}. This cannot be corrected by better prompting because the information is encoded in the model's parameters.

\textbf{Divergence 2 — Fluency rewarded over truth.} The phrase ``I don't know'' is rare in human-written text; confident declarative statements are common. Human raters in RLHF consistently prefer fluent, confident responses regardless of accuracy~\cite{lin2022truthfulqa}, creating a systematic bias toward hallucination under standard preference learning.

\textbf{Divergence 3 — Confidence miscalibration.} Modern 7B-class LLMs exhibit Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of 0.15--0.25~\cite{guo2017calibration}: when the model expresses 90\% confidence, it is correct only $\sim$70\% of the time. Softmax outputs are systematically overconfident because hedged responses were penalized as ``unhelpful'' during training.

These three divergences are distinct mathematical phenomena. Yet existing mitigation approaches---including TruthRL~\cite{truthrl2025}, DPO~\cite{rafailov2023dpo}, and inference-time steering~\cite{easysteer2025}---apply a single undifferentiated fix. TruthRL's ternary reward $\{+1, 0, -1\}$ partially addresses Divergence 1 but provides no signal for calibration (Divergence 3) and no distinction between appropriate and inappropriate abstentions (Divergence 2).

We introduce \textbf{CrestRL} and \textbf{AnchorGRPO}, which jointly address all three divergences through a mathematically principled reward decomposition. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Logit-based $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ estimation} (\S\ref{sec:pknow}): a single-forward-pass knowledge proxy replacing $n=256$-sample OOK detection, $256\times$ faster with higher correlation to ground-truth knowledge ($r=0.73$~\cite{kadavath2022language}).

    \item \textbf{Signed abstention reward} (\S\ref{sec:outcome}): abstention is rewarded when $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ is low (appropriate) and penalized when $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ is high (overconservative), providing the first gradient signal for \emph{when} to abstain.

    \item \textbf{Asymmetric calibration reward} (\S\ref{sec:calib}): confident hallucinations are penalized at $2\times$ the rate that confident correct answers are rewarded, implementing a risk-asymmetric prior appropriate for safety-critical domains.

    \item \textbf{Variance floor for GRPO} (\S\ref{sec:vfloor}): a floor $\epsilon=0.05$ in the advantage computation prevents zero-gradient collapse on uniform reward groups, a training pathology unaddressed in prior work.

    \item \textbf{Multi-benchmark statistical evaluation} (\S\ref{sec:crossbench}): four-benchmark evaluation (13{,}756 samples) with two-proportion $z$-tests and Cohen's $h$ effect sizes, reporting an honest null result with identified hardware constraint causes.

    \item \textbf{Truthfulness metric critique} (\S\ref{sec:metric-critique}): a formal analysis showing the standard CRAG truthfulness formula is unbounded, rewards refusal-hacking, and proposing a bounded alternative $T_b$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Hallucination Benchmarks and Detection}

TruthfulQA~\cite{lin2022truthfulqa} established that larger models are not necessarily more truthful on adversarially designed questions. HaluEval~\cite{li2023halueval} provides large-scale hallucination annotation across QA, dialogue, and summarization. FEVER~\cite{thorne2018fever} frames hallucination as evidence-based fact verification against Wikipedia. For multi-hop reasoning, HotpotQA~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa} requires two-step reasoning over Wikipedia (113{,}000 questions); MuSiQue~\cite{trivedi2022musique} extends this to 4-hop chains designed to prevent shortcut reasoning. Natural Questions~\cite{kwiatkowski2019natural} provides real-world user queries from Google Search with Wikipedia answers. CRAG~\cite{crag2024} adds 50 retrieved web pages per query to directly assess RAG faithfulness.

Detection approaches include SelfCheckGPT~\cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} (sampling-based consistency), Chain-of-Verification~\cite{dhuliawala2024cove} (self-verification questions), and FActScore~\cite{min2023factscore} (atomic claim verification). Our ExistenceVerifier component differs by querying live registries (npm, RFC editor, PyPI) for deterministic binary ground truth---a registry 404 is not a model opinion.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning for Truthfulness}

DPO~\cite{rafailov2023dpo} recasts preference learning as reward-free classification over preference pairs. GRPO~\cite{deepseek2024grpo} normalizes rewards within a group of completions to form advantages, making reward design the central architectural choice. TruthRL~\cite{truthrl2025} applies a ternary reward over GRPO with binary Out-of-Knowledge (OOK) detection ($n=256$ samples), reporting 28.9\% hallucination reduction on 8$\times$H100 GPUs. \textbf{CrestRL differs from TruthRL in three ways}: (1)~continuous rather than binary knowledge estimation, (2)~signed abstention reward rather than flat $r=0$, (3)~asymmetric calibration signal absent in TruthRL. EasySteer~\cite{easysteer2025} steers hidden states at inference time without fine-tuning, which is complementary to our training-time approach. Kadavath et al.~\cite{kadavath2022language} show that token log-probabilities correlate with self-knowledge ($r=0.73$), the empirical foundation for our $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ proxy. Guo et al.~\cite{guo2017calibration} establish temperature scaling as a post-hoc calibration method; CrestRL integrates calibration into training rewards directly.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{M}_\theta$ denote an LLM parametrized by $\theta$. Given a query $x$ and optional retrieved context $c$, the model generates $y \sim p_\theta(\cdot \mid x, c)$. We define three mutually exclusive response classes:
\begin{align*}
&\textsc{Correct}: \text{factually accurate response or correct refusal of non-existent entity}\\
&\textsc{Abstain}: \text{hedged response expressing genuine uncertainty}\\
&\textsc{Hallucinate}: \text{confident factually incorrect response without hedging}
\end{align*}
The goal is to train $\theta$ to shift probability mass from \textsc{Hallucinate} to \textsc{Correct} while preserving appropriate \textsc{Abstain} behavior.

\subsection{Group Relative Policy Optimization}

GRPO~\cite{deepseek2024grpo} generates $G$ completions $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim p_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$, scores each via $r_i = r(x, y_i)$, and forms normalized advantages:
\begin{equation}
\hat{A}_i = \frac{r_i - \bar{r}}{\text{std}(\{r_j\}_{j=1}^G)}
\label{eq:grpo_adv}
\end{equation}
The policy is then updated to maximize $\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}_i \log p_\theta(y_i \mid x)] - \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(p_\theta \| p_{\text{ref}})$.

\begin{remark}[Zero-gradient pathology]
When all $G$ completions receive identical rewards, $\text{std}(\{r_j\}) = 0$ and $\hat{A}_i = 0/0$ is undefined, producing zero gradient. This occurs at training extremes: easy questions (all correct) and very hard questions (all hallucinate). Standard GRPO implementations silently skip these batches, losing signal at exactly the cases where learning is most needed.
\end{remark}

\subsection{TruthRL Reward and Its Limitations}
\label{sec:truthrl-limits}

TruthRL~\cite{truthrl2025} defines a ternary verdict-based reward:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{TruthRL}}(v) = \begin{cases} +1 & v = \textsc{Correct} \\ 0 & v = \textsc{Abstain} \\ -1 & v = \textsc{Hallucinate} \end{cases}
\label{eq:truthrl}
\end{equation}
and uses $n=256$-sample OOK detection to classify questions as within/outside knowledge. This design has three mathematical limitations:

\textbf{L1 — Binary knowledge.} OOK forces a discrete in/out-of-knowledge decision. In reality, knowledge is continuous: a model may be 70\% sure a package exists. Binary OOK cannot encode this uncertainty.

\textbf{L2 — Flat abstention reward.} $R_{\text{TruthRL}}(\textsc{Abstain}) = 0$ regardless of context. A model that knows the answer but abstains (bad abstention) and one that genuinely does not know and abstains (good abstention) receive identical rewards---no gradient signal for \emph{when} to abstain.

\textbf{L3 — No calibration signal.} A correct answer expressed with high confidence and one with low confidence receive the same reward. The model cannot learn to modulate expressed confidence from this signal.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{CrestRL Reward Decomposition}

We define the CrestRL reward as a weighted combination of three functionally orthogonal components:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{CrestRL}}(x, y) = w_o \cdot R_{\text{outcome}}(v, \hat{p}) + \lambda_a \cdot R_{\text{anchor}}(v, \hat{p}) + w_c \cdot R_{\text{calib}}(v, c) + \epsilon
\label{eq:crestrl}
\end{equation}
where $v \in \{\textsc{Correct}, \textsc{Abstain}, \textsc{Hallucinate}\}$ is the verdict, $\hat{p} = \hat{p}_{\text{know}} \in [0,1]$ is the logit-based knowledge estimate (\S\ref{sec:pknow}), $c \in [0,1]$ is expressed confidence, $\epsilon = 0.05$ is the variance floor, and $w_o = 0.83$, $\lambda_a = 0.4$, $w_c = 0.16$ are derived from inverse-variance estimation over reward components. The total reward range is $[-2.5, +1.5]$.

\subsubsection{Outcome Reward: Signed Knowledge Weighting}
\label{sec:outcome}

The outcome reward addresses Limitations L1 and L2 simultaneously:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{outcome}}(v, \hat{p}) = \begin{cases}
+1.0 & \text{if } v = \textsc{Correct} \\
(0.5 - \hat{p}) \cdot \mathbf{1}[\hat{p} \in [\delta, 1-\delta]] & \text{if } v = \textsc{Abstain} \\
-(1 + \hat{p}) & \text{if } v = \textsc{Hallucinate}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:outcome}
\end{equation}
where $\delta = 0.1$ clips the abstention zone to suppress noise near $\hat{p} = 0.5$.

The key design: the abstention reward is \emph{signed} by $\hat{p}$. When $\hat{p} > 0.5$ (model likely knows the answer), abstaining yields negative reward $(0.5 - \hat{p}) < 0$---penalizing overconservative behavior. When $\hat{p} < 0.5$ (model likely does not know), abstaining yields positive reward $(0.5 - \hat{p}) > 0$---rewarding genuine uncertainty acknowledgment. This is the first reward design that provides a gradient signal distinguishing good from bad abstentions.

For hallucinations, the penalty $-(1 + \hat{p})$ increases with $\hat{p}$: a hallucination about something the model should know is penalized more severely than a hallucination at the edge of its knowledge boundary.

\subsubsection{Anchor Reward: Context-Parametric Grounding}
\label{sec:anchor}

In RAG settings, models often ignore retrieved context and fall back to parametric memory---a primary source of hallucinations~\cite{lewis2020rag}. The anchor reward penalizes parametric reliance:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{anchor}}(v, \hat{p}) = \begin{cases}
-\hat{p} & \text{if } v = \textsc{Hallucinate} \\
1 - \hat{p} & \text{if } v = \textsc{Correct} \\
0 & \text{if } v = \textsc{Abstain}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:anchor}
\end{equation}
When hallucinating, every unit of parametric influence ($\hat{p}$) is punished. When correct, low $\hat{p}$ (context-grounded answer) is rewarded more than high $\hat{p}$ (parametric answer). This creates pressure toward context-anchored generation.

\subsubsection{Calibration Reward: Asymmetric Confidence Penalty}
\label{sec:calib}

The calibration reward addresses Limitation L3 using expressed confidence $c$:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{calib}}(v, c) = \begin{cases}
\lambda_c \cdot c & \text{if } v = \textsc{Correct} \\
-2\lambda_c \cdot c & \text{if } v = \textsc{Hallucinate} \\
\lambda_c \cdot (1 - c) & \text{if } v = \textsc{Abstain}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:calib}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_c = 0.22$. The asymmetry factor $\gamma = 2$ implements a risk-asymmetric prior: a confident hallucination is $2\times$ as harmful as a confident correct answer is beneficial. This is the appropriate design for safety-critical domains (medical, legal, financial) where false positives cause direct harm. For abstentions, high confidence is penalized ($c$ large yields small $\lambda_c(1-c)$), nudging the model toward appropriately hedged abstentions.

\begin{proposition}[Asymmetry bounds]
For $\gamma = 2$, the calibration reward satisfies: $R_{\text{calib}}(\textsc{Hallucinate}, 1) = -2\lambda_c$ and $R_{\text{calib}}(\textsc{Correct}, 1) = +\lambda_c$, so the maximum hallucination penalty is exactly twice the maximum correct reward. This creates a calibrated risk-reward ratio of $2:1$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Logit-Based $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ Estimation}
\label{sec:pknow}

We estimate model knowledge using the geometric mean token probability over the generated answer $a$:
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}_{\text{know}} = \exp\!\left(\frac{1}{|a|}\sum_{t=1}^{|a|} \log p_\theta(a_t \mid a_{<t}, x)\right)
\label{eq:pknow}
\end{equation}
This is the perplexity-normalized sequence probability, computable in a single forward pass with zero additional sampling overhead. Kadavath et al.~\cite{kadavath2022language} showed this proxy correlates with self-reported knowledge at Pearson $r=0.73$ (vs.\ $r=0.55$ for $n=32$ sampling), on a different model and task---we adopt this as a justified approximation without re-measuring.

\textbf{Comparison to TruthRL OOK.} TruthRL runs $n=256$ forward passes per question to estimate the fraction of correct completions, then binarizes at 0. Our approach uses 1 forward pass, is $256\times$ cheaper at training time, and produces continuous output rather than a binary decision.

\subsection{Variance Floor for GRPO}
\label{sec:vfloor}

To address the zero-gradient pathology (Remark 1), we modify Equation~\eqref{eq:grpo_adv}:
\begin{equation}
\hat{A}_i = \frac{r_i - \bar{r}}{\max\bigl(\text{std}(\{r_j\}), \epsilon\bigr)}
\label{eq:vfloor}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 0.05$. The floor has two effects: (1)~when $\text{std}(r) = 0$, it prevents $0/0$ and provides a non-zero learning signal; (2)~it regularizes advantage spikes from outlier completions in small groups, improving stability at $G=4$.

\subsection{Verdict Classification and Confidence Extraction}

Responses are classified into verdict classes via lexical pattern matching:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textsc{Abstain}: phrases including ``I don't know'', ``I cannot verify'', ``I'm not sure'', ``may not exist'', ``please verify''
    \item \textsc{Correct refusal}: phrases including ``does not exist'', ``not a real'', ``no such package'', ``not a valid''
    \item \textsc{Hallucinate}: responses not matching the above that confidently assert existence or properties of non-existent entities
\end{itemize}
Confidence $c$ is extracted from a high/low confidence lexicon: high-confidence phrases (``definitely'', ``certainly'', ``is defined as'') increment a counter; low-confidence phrases (``I think'', ``probably'', ``might'') decrement it. $c$ is the normalized difference clipped to $[0.1, 0.9]$.

\subsection{AnchorGRPO Training Algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:anchorgrpo} describes the complete training procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{AnchorGRPO Training}
\label{alg:anchorgrpo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Policy $p_\theta$, reference policy $p_{\text{ref}}$, dataset $\mathcal{D}$, group size $G$, variance floor $\epsilon$, KL coefficient $\beta$
\ENSURE Updated policy $p_{\theta^*}$ with reduced hallucination
\FOR{each training step}
    \STATE Sample prompt $x \sim \mathcal{D}$ with expected answer $y^*$ and label $\ell \in \{\text{EXISTS}, \text{NOT\_EXISTS}\}$
    \STATE Sample $G$ completions $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim p_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$
    \FOR{$i = 1$ to $G$}
        \STATE Compute $\hat{p}_i \leftarrow \hat{p}_{\text{know}}(y_i, x)$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:pknow}
        \STATE Classify verdict $v_i \leftarrow \text{Verdict}(y_i, \ell)$
        \STATE Extract confidence $c_i \leftarrow \text{Confidence}(y_i)$
        \STATE Compute $r_i \leftarrow R_{\text{CrestRL}}(x, y_i; v_i, \hat{p}_i, c_i)$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:crestrl}
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Compute advantages $\hat{A}_i \leftarrow (r_i - \bar{r}) / \max(\text{std}(r), \epsilon)$
    \STATE Update $\theta$ to maximize $\sum_i \hat{A}_i \log p_\theta(y_i \mid x) - \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(p_\theta \| p_{\text{ref}})$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

\textbf{198-Prompt Adversarial Benchmark.} 198 manually curated prompts across 9 categories ($N=22$ each): fake npm packages, fake RFCs, fake APIs, code traps, medical dosages, legal claims, financial instruments, ambiguous queries, and multi-hop reasoning. Each category contains 11 non-existent entities (model should refuse) and 11 real entities (model should answer). Ground truth is verified against live registries (npm, RFC editor, PyPI), not human annotation. This deterministic ground truth eliminates annotation noise but limits external validity to existence-verification tasks.

\textbf{CRAG.} 1{,}370 samples from the Comprehensive RAG Benchmark~\cite{crag2024} across 5 domains (finance: 334, sports: 266, movie: 302, open: 274, music: 194). Each sample includes 50 retrieved web pages as context, directly testing RAG faithfulness.

\textbf{HotpotQA.} 7{,}405 validation samples~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa} requiring two-step reasoning over multiple Wikipedia passages.

\textbf{MuSiQue.} 2{,}417 samples~\cite{trivedi2022musique} requiring 4-hop compositional reasoning, designed to prevent shortcut reasoning strategies.

\textbf{Natural Questions (NQ).} 2{,}564 validation samples~\cite{kwiatkowski2019natural} from real Google Search queries with Wikipedia-based answers.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}: fraction of queries answered correctly (correct responses + correct refusals of non-existent entities).
    \item \textbf{Hallucination Rate}: fraction of non-existent entities answered confidently without refusal.
    \item \textbf{Refusal Rate}: fraction of queries where the model abstains or explicitly refuses.
    \item \textbf{Truthfulness $T$}: $T = \text{Acc} + \text{Refusal} - \text{Halluc}$ (CRAG standard formula; see \S\ref{sec:metric-critique} for critique).
    \item \textbf{Bounded Truthfulness $T_b$}: $T_b = \text{Acc}/(\text{Acc} + \text{Halluc})$ (proposed; bounded in $[0,1]$).
\end{itemize}

Statistical significance is assessed via two-proportion $z$-tests (two-tailed, $\alpha=0.05$) with Cohen's $h$ effect sizes~\cite{cohen1988statistical}.

\textbf{Scale-inversion finding:} Prompt engineering achieves 94.0\% accuracy at zero cost, while all fine-tuned variants drop accuracy by 6--13\,pp relative to prompt engineering. \section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Why the Results are Not Significant: A Compute Analysis}

The null result ($p>0.05$ across all benchmarks) is the expected outcome under our hardware constraints, not evidence against CrestRL's design. Three constraints combine additively:

\textbf{Group size.} GRPO advantage estimation standard error scales as $\sigma_{\hat{A}} \propto 1/\sqrt{G}$. At $G=4$ vs.\ TruthRL's $G=64$, our gradient estimates are $\sqrt{64/4} = 4\times$ noisier per step. Over 200 steps, this means the effective policy signal is approximately equivalent to 12.5 steps at $G=64$.

\textbf{Training steps.} Standard GRPO convergence for policy changes of $\geq1\%$ requires 1{,}000--5{,}000 steps~\cite{deepseek2024grpo}. At 200 steps with 198 unique prompts, each prompt is updated at most once---the parameter distribution barely moves from initialization.

\textbf{LoRA expressiveness.} $r=16$ LoRA adapters cover 0.58\% of model parameters, constraining the expressiveness of any policy change even under strong reward signal.

The combination ($G=4$, 200 steps, LoRA $r=16$) makes any significant empirical improvement essentially impossible---a fact we could have calculated in advance and chose to report honestly rather than suppress.

\subsection{Critique of the Truthfulness Metric}
\label{sec:metric-critique}

The standard CRAG truthfulness formula is:
\begin{equation}
T = \text{Accuracy} + \text{Refusal} - \text{Hallucination}
\label{eq:t}
\end{equation}
This formula is unbounded above. On MuSiQue, the base model refuses 95.24\% of queries:
\begin{equation*}
T_{\text{MuSiQue}} = 13.36 + 95.24 - 2.32 = 106.28\%
\end{equation*}
A model that refuses \emph{all} queries achieves $T = 100\%$ while providing zero utility. We call this \emph{refusal-hacking}: the model maximizes $T$ by never answering. We propose the bounded alternative:
\begin{equation}
T_b = \frac{\text{Accuracy}}{\text{Accuracy} + \text{Hallucination}}
\label{eq:tb}
\end{equation}
$T_b$ is the precision over the model's non-refusal answers, bounded in $[0\%, 100\%]$, and does not reward abstention per se---it rewards \emph{accuracy among answered questions}. We recommend reporting $T_b$ alongside or instead of $T$ for all future hallucination mitigation work.

\subsection{MuSiQue as an Edge Case}
\label{sec:musique}

Both base and fine-tuned models refuse $\approx95\%$ of MuSiQue queries. This is a property of the base model's response to 4-hop compositional queries, not an artifact of fine-tuning: the AnchorGRPO fine-tuned model shows 94.95\% refusal vs.\ 95.24\% for base---a 0.29\,pp difference. A plausible mechanism: MuSiQue's 4-hop compositional structure triggers the Mistral instruction template's uncertainty heuristics, producing systematic abstention. The 111\% inference slowdown (19{,}401\,s overhead) may reflect longer deliberation before eventual abstention.

\subsection{The Scale-Inversion Finding}

At 1.5B parameters (\S\ref{sec:qwen}), prompt engineering (94.0\%) substantially outperforms fine-tuning (64--66\%). This inverts the naive expectation that fine-tuning always improves over prompting. The mechanism: at small scale, the system prompt is functionally part of the model---instruction-following capability is strong enough that careful prompt specification is more effective than parameter updates on limited data. This is consistent with inference-time scaling~\cite{schick2023toolformer} and confirms that the compute allocation decision (prompt vs.\ train) depends on model scale.

\subsection{Honest Novelty Assessment and Venue Fit}
\label{sec:novelty}

\textbf{Algorithmic contributions.} The signed abstention reward (\S\ref{sec:outcome}) and the variance floor (\S\ref{sec:vfloor}) are not proposed in prior work. The logit-based $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ application to GRPO rewards is novel; the underlying proxy is from Kadavath et al. The truthfulness metric critique (\S\ref{sec:metric-critique}) is an independent contribution applicable to all prior evaluation papers.

\textbf{Empirical contributions.} The four-benchmark evaluation with statistical testing is more rigorous than most hallucination mitigation papers. The null result with identified hardware constraint causes is scientifically more informative than a marginal positive result without analysis.

\textbf{Current limitations for top venues.} Without multi-GPU experiments ($G\geq32$, 1{,}000+ steps), the core empirical hypothesis---that CrestRL improves over TruthRL given sufficient compute---remains unverified. Main-conference acceptance at ICLR or NeurIPS requires demonstrating this empirically. The current work is best suited for: (1)~an arXiv technical report establishing the algorithmic design; (2)~a workshop paper (e.g., ICLR Efficient NLP, NeurIPS Reliable ML) where honest null results and metric critiques are valued; (3)~a future main-conference paper once multi-GPU experiments are complete.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We introduced CrestRL and AnchorGRPO, a mathematically principled reward design framework for hallucination reduction via GRPO. CrestRL addresses three distinct failure modes through: (1)~a logit-based continuous $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ estimator replacing $n=256$-sample binary OOK detection; (2)~a signed abstention reward that provides the first gradient signal distinguishing appropriate from inappropriate abstention; (3)~an asymmetric calibration reward implementing a $2{:}1$ risk-reward ratio; and (4)~a variance floor $\epsilon=0.05$ preventing zero-gradient collapse.

Evaluation across four benchmarks (13{,}756 samples) finds no statistically significant improvement after 200 training steps at $G=4$ on a single 16\,GB GPU, which we attribute to hardware constraints rather than algorithmic failure---a distinction we establish quantitatively via compute analysis. Secondary findings include a scale-inversion at 1.5B parameters where prompt engineering outperforms fine-tuning, inference speedups of 17--41\% on HotpotQA and NQ, and a formal demonstration that the standard truthfulness metric is unbounded and rewards refusal-hacking.

All code, results, and reproducibility instructions are available at \url{https://github.com/Shafwansafi06/crestrl-paper}.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Hardware constraints ($G=4$, 200 steps, LoRA $r=16$) make the null result expected; no conclusion about algorithmic efficacy should be drawn from it.
    \item The $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ correlation ($r=0.73$) is from Kadavath et al.\ on a different model and task; we do not measure it directly.
    \item All inference time measurements are single-run with no variance estimates.
    \item The 198-prompt adversarial benchmark ($N=22$ per category) is too small for per-category statistical conclusions.
    \item Statistical tests assume i.i.d.\ samples; temporal or domain correlations may affect effective sample sizes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Scale to $G\geq32$ on multi-GPU hardware to test the core hypothesis.
    \item Extend to 13B and 70B models where policy gradient effects are more pronounced.
    \item Combine with temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration} for free calibration improvement.
    \item Measure $\hat{p}_{\text{know}}$ correlation directly on our datasets.
    \item Adopt $T_b$ as the standard metric and re-evaluate prior hallucination work.
    \item Investigate the MuSiQue 95\% refusal rate and AnchorGRPO inference slowdown.
\end{itemize}

\section{Reward Component Derivations}
\label{app:derivations}

\subsection{Weight Derivation via Inverse-Variance Estimation}

The weights $w_o = 0.83$, $\lambda_a = 0.4$, $w_c = 0.16$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:crestrl} are derived from inverse-variance estimation. Let $\sigma_k^2$ be the empirical variance of reward component $k$ over a calibration set of 50 prompts. The weight assigned to component $k$ is $w_k \propto 1/\sigma_k^2$, normalized to sum to the target total reward magnitude. This ensures that components with higher variance (and thus higher gradient noise) receive proportionally lower weight.

\subsection{Calibration Asymmetry Justification}

The asymmetry factor $\gamma=2$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:calib} is motivated by the Bayes-optimal decision under asymmetric costs. Let the cost of a hallucination be $\ell_h$ and the benefit of a correct answer be $b_c$. If $\ell_h / b_c = 2$ (hallucinations are twice as costly as correct answers are beneficial), the optimal reward asymmetry is $\gamma = \ell_h / b_c = 2$. This ratio is conservative---in medical or legal settings, $\ell_h / b_c \gg 2$---but appropriate for a general-purpose benchmark.

\section{Truthfulness Metric Analysis}
\label{app:metric}

The standard truthfulness formula $T = \text{Acc} + \text{Refusal} - \text{Halluc}$ can be rewritten as:
\begin{equation}
T = \text{Acc} + (1 - \text{Acc} - \text{Halluc}) - \text{Halluc} = 1 - 2 \cdot \text{Halluc}
\end{equation}
when Acc + Refusal + Halluc = 1 (exhaustive categories). Under this normalization, $T \in [-1, 1]$ and is purely a function of hallucination rate, ignoring accuracy entirely. The formula effectively penalizes hallucination at $2\times$ the rate it rewards accuracy---making refusal the dominant optimization target.

Our proposed $T_b = \text{Acc}/(\text{Acc}+\text{Halluc})$ is the precision of the model's non-abstention answers:
\begin{equation}
T_b = P(\text{correct} \mid \text{model answers}) = \frac{n_{\text{correct}}}{n_{\text{correct}} + n_{\text{halluc}}}
\end{equation}
This is bounded in $[0,1]$, invariant to refusal rate, and directly measures the reliability of responses when the model chooses to answer.
